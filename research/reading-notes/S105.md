## 1. Core Claim
- Most memory-augmented agents treat memory construction, retrieval, and utilization as isolated subroutines, leading to two coupled problems: strategic blindness (local heuristics instead of explicit reasoning) on the forward path, and sparse, delayed supervision on the backward path.
- Coordinating the entire memory cycle through multi-agent reasoning and in-situ self-evolution yields consistent performance gains across multiple backbones and storage backends.

## 2. Method / Mechanism
- **Multi-agent framework**:
  - *Meta-Thinker*: Produces structured strategic guidance.
  - *Memory Manager*: Steers memory construction under guidance.
  - *Query Reasoner*: Performs iterative retrieval directed by strategy.
- **Forward path**: Strategic guidance coordinates construction and retrieval.
- **Backward path**: In-situ self-evolving memory construction synthesizes probe QA pairs, verifies current memory, and converts failures into repair actions before finalization.
- **Plug-and-play**: Works with three different storage backends without modification.

## 3. Key Evidence
- Extensive experiments on **LoCoMo** show consistent outperformance of existing baselines across multiple LLM backbones.
- Improves three different storage backends in a plug-and-play manner, demonstrating backend-agnostic benefits.
- Ablation data (numeric claims from tables) show gains from strategic active retrieval vs. unguided active retrieval.

## 4. Limitations & Gaps
- Multi-agent coordination introduces additional inference calls (Meta-Thinker, Memory Manager, Query Reasoner), potentially increasing latency.
- Evaluated only on LoCoMo; no evidence on task-oriented or coding agents.
- The in-situ self-evolution mechanism depends on generating high-quality probe QA pairs, which may fail for novel or sparse domains.

## 5. Relevance to Our Review
- **Academic memory architectures**: A leading example of multi-agent coordination for memory management.
- **Hierarchical memory**: The Meta-Thinker → Manager → Reasoner hierarchy mirrors control-policy layers in memory systems.
